# Finding: Column-Based First-Person Renderer

## Summary

The reference engine renders the player's view as a vertical-column projection: the framebuffer is filled one screen column at a time, where each column corresponds to a single ray direction sampled from a fixed horizontal field of view. Wall column heights come from a perpendicular-distance / focal-length formula with explicit fisheye correction. Floors and ceilings are drawn as horizontal spans inside the gaps left by walls, with per-row distance derived from screen Y. Lighting is implemented as a precomputed colormap table indexed by (sector light level, distance band), plus a small one-shade adjustment that brightens or darkens entire wall segments based on which map axis they run along. Wall traversal in the reference uses a precomputed binary space partition (BSP) tree, but the per-column projection math is independent of the traversal strategy and reduces cleanly to a grid-DDA walk when the world is a uniform tile array.

## Observed Mechanics

### Column Projection Model

- **Behavior**: The viewport is rendered as one vertical column per screen X coordinate. Each X maps to one view direction angle; the column's pixel content is determined entirely by what that single ray hits.
- **Rules**:
  - At startup, two parallel lookup tables are built: one mapping every fine-grained view angle to the screen X it lands on, and the inverse mapping every screen X to the smallest view angle whose ray reaches that column.
  - The horizontal field of view spans one quarter of a full circle (90°). It is encoded as a count of fine-angle steps where the angle table holds 8192 steps for a full circle, so the field-of-view count is 2048 fine-angle steps.
  - The projection focal length is computed once as `focal = screen_half_width / tan(half_fov)`. Because the chosen FOV is 90°, this simplifies to `focal = screen_half_width` in the reference's nominal pixel units.
  - For each screen X, the per-column ray angle is `camera_yaw + column_offset[x]`, where the per-column offset table was generated by inverting the tangent-based focal-length mapping above.
- **Constants**:
  - Framebuffer: 320 columns wide × 200 rows tall (4:3 display aspect; pixels are non-square — slightly taller than wide on a CRT, which the renderer compensates for via separate horizontal/vertical scale factors).
  - Horizontal FOV: 90°.
  - Fine-angle resolution: 8192 steps per full circle (the angle-to-tangent and tangent-to-angle tables are sized for this).
  - Status-bar carve-out: full screen height minus a fixed bottom strip (≈ 32 rows in the reference) is the actual render viewport; centerY is half the viewport height.
- **Feel**: One ray per column gives the unmistakable "flat" / "pixel-stretched" first-person look of the genre. Doubling or halving the column count trades horizontal resolution against fill cost linearly.

### Perpendicular Distance (per Wall Segment)

- **Behavior**: The distance value used to scale a wall's column heights is the *perpendicular* distance from the camera to the wall's infinite line — not the ray-distance from the camera to the column's particular hit point on the wall.
- **Rules**:
  - For each visible wall segment, the renderer first computes the camera-to-segment-endpoint distance (a plain Euclidean length).
  - It then projects that hypotenuse onto the segment's normal via a sine of the offset angle between the wall normal and the ray to the endpoint, yielding a perpendicular distance.
  - This perpendicular distance is computed once per segment, independent of column count, then reused inside the column loop.
  - The per-column scale formula is `scale = focal × sin(B) / (perp_distance × sin(A))`, where A is the angle between the column's view ray and the camera forward axis (so `sin(A) = cos(off_axis)` after a 90° offset), and B is the angle between the column's view ray and the wall normal. The first sine handles the off-axis fisheye correction; the second handles the obliqueness of the wall to the line of sight.
  - Equivalently in a grid-walk implementation: take the axis-projected ray distance (the X- or Y-component depending on which axis the ray crossed last to enter the wall tile), which already gives the perpendicular distance for an axis-aligned tile world. Then divide focal by that distance to get column scale, with no further trig needed.
- **Constants**:
  - Per-column scale is clamped to a fixed-point range. The reference clamps to roughly `[1/256, 64×]` of the unit projection, which prevents extremely close walls from producing astronomically large columns and extremely distant ones from producing zero-height columns. The clamp acts as both a "near plane" and a "far plane" for the column math.
- **Feel**: The same wall, viewed off-axis, produces a uniformly slanted column of pixels rather than a curved one. This is the single most important step for "looks like a real first-person view" rather than "looks like a fisheye lens."

### Fisheye Correction

- **Behavior**: Without correction, walls would visibly bow outward at the edges of the screen because the ray distance to a flat wall grows secant-style as the ray angle moves away from the camera forward axis. The reference cancels this by dividing by the cosine of the off-axis angle.
- **Rules**:
  - Per-column scale formula carries a `sin(A)` term in the denominator, where A is offset from the wall normal in the reference's parameterization. Because `A = 90° + (column_angle − camera_forward_angle)`, `sin(A) = cos(column_angle − camera_forward_angle)` — i.e. the cosine of how far off-axis the column is.
  - In a grid-walk implementation the equivalent step is: store the *perpendicular-axis component* of the ray's travel rather than the Euclidean distance. Both formulations produce identical pixel output up to fixed-point rounding.
- **Constants**:
  - At a 90° FOV the cosine factor varies between 1.0 (centerline column) and roughly cos(45°) ≈ 0.707 (extreme edge columns). Edge walls are therefore drawn ~1.41× taller than they would be with naive ray-distance, exactly cancelling the secant blowup.
- **Feel**: Walls are perceived as flat; corners look like real 3D corners rather than fisheye-stretched curves.

### NS-vs-EW Wall Shading (Fake Directional Light)

- **Behavior**: A small per-segment integer adjustment to the chosen light table brightens or darkens entire wall segments based on which map axis they run along. This produces the impression of a single directional light source without computing real surface normals.
- **Rules**:
  - For each visible wall segment, the renderer takes the sector's nominal light level, then:
    - if the segment runs along one map axis (its two endpoints share one coordinate): subtract one shade — the segment is rendered slightly darker than nominal.
    - else if the segment runs along the *other* map axis (endpoints share the other coordinate): add one shade — slightly brighter.
    - else (segment is at an oblique angle): no adjustment.
  - The adjustment is one integer index step into the same per-distance shade table used for nominal lighting; it is *not* a separate lighting pass.
  - The convention in the reference: walls extending east–west (faces pointing north or south) are darker; walls extending north–south (faces pointing east or west) are brighter. Either convention works — pick one and use it consistently.
- **Constants**:
  - One light-level step difference between "darker axis" and nominal, and one step between nominal and "brighter axis" — i.e. a total spread of two shade steps between the two axis groups.
- **Feel**: Gives a flat tile world a sense of depth and orientation cues "for free" — the player can tell a corner from a long straight wall by the brightness change, even with everything textured (or coloured) identically.

### Distance Attenuation (Light / Fog Falloff)

- **Behavior**: Walls and floors darken with distance via a precomputed colormap table, not via per-pixel arithmetic. A surface at distance D in a sector at light level L is drawn through colormap row `colormap_table[L][D_band]`.
- **Rules**:
  - The colormap table is indexed by (sector light level, distance band).
  - Sector light has 16 discrete levels.
  - There are 32 brightness steps in the master colormap; the distance-band index selects one of those 32 steps for a given (level, distance) pair.
  - For walls, the distance band is derived from the per-column scale value (closer columns have larger scale → smaller band index → brighter). The conversion is `band = scale >> wall_band_shift`, clamped to `[0, wall_band_count - 1]`. The reference uses 48 bands and a 12-bit shift.
  - For floors and ceilings, the distance band is derived from the per-row distance directly: `band = distance >> z_band_shift`, clamped to `[0, z_band_count - 1]`. The reference uses 128 bands and a 20-bit shift on the fixed-point distance.
  - There is also a small "extra light" bias added globally per frame (e.g. brightened briefly during a weapon discharge) — a single integer offset applied before the level/band lookup.
  - When the player is in an "invulnerability" state, a special fixed colormap is selected per-frame that overrides the entire (level, distance) lookup with one full-bright inverted palette.
- **Constants**:
  - Sector light levels: 16.
  - Master colormap brightness steps: 32 (index 0 = full bright, increasing index = darker).
  - Wall distance-band count: 48; wall band-derivation shift: 12 bits applied to the per-column scale.
  - Floor/ceiling distance-band count: 128; floor/ceiling band-derivation shift: 20 bits applied to the per-row distance.
  - Distance divisor (an extra "atmosphere thickness" knob): 2 — applied as a divide on the band index before clamping. Larger values mean less aggressive falloff with distance.
- **Feel**: Distant walls fade into darkness smoothly without per-pixel cost; the discrete shade steps are imperceptible at the master colormap's 32 levels but cheap to evaluate.

### Floor and Ceiling Treatment

- **Behavior**: The space inside a column that is *not* covered by a wall is filled with floor (below the wall) and ceiling (above the wall). The reference textures these with a perspective-correct horizontal-span technique; a simplified renderer can fill them with two solid colours (floor colour for rows below the horizon, ceiling colour for rows above) and reuse all the wall-projection code unchanged.
- **Rules** (textured floor/ceiling, the reference's actual approach):
  - After all walls in a frame are drawn, the renderer walks the leftover top and bottom regions per column and groups columns into "horizontal flat regions" — every contiguous run of columns whose top-of-floor-region row and bottom-of-floor-region row are similar.
  - Each flat region is then rasterized row-by-row. For each row:
    - Per-row distance `d = plane_height × row_depth[y]`, where `row_depth[y] = screen_half_width / |y − center_y|` and `plane_height` is the absolute vertical distance between the camera and the floor (or ceiling) plane. This single value is constant along the row.
    - Per-row sample step in world space is `d × base_step_x` and `d × base_step_y`, where the base steps are derived from the camera's right-vector and forward-vector at frame start. Walking the row in screen X then corresponds to a fixed step in world XY coordinates — a classic affine span fill.
    - Per-row lighting band is `d >> z_band_shift`, indexed into the same colormap table as walls.
  - Sky is a special case: a chosen "sky" texture is sampled column-by-column where the column index is `(camera_yaw + column_offset[x]) >> sky_shift`, and is always rendered at full brightness regardless of distance.
- **Rules** (simplified flat-colour floor/ceiling, used when textures are not loaded):
  - Above the horizon (rows < center_y): fill with the chosen ceiling colour.
  - Below the horizon (rows ≥ center_y): fill with the chosen floor colour.
  - Skip the per-row distance / texture-step computations entirely; the column loop only writes wall pixels.
  - Optional: still apply per-row distance attenuation to the flat colour for a "fog" effect.
- **Constants**:
  - Per-row depth table: precomputed at viewport-size change time, length = viewport height.
  - Span fill rate: one row of pixels per per-row span-rasterizer call; texture lookup is one byte per pixel; rate scales with viewport width × number of rows owned by the plane.
- **Feel**: Textured floors give strong forward-motion cues (the floor texture appears to flow under the camera). Solid-colour floors are functional but sterile — the loss is mostly perceptual richness, not mechanic. A consumer choosing solid colours should pick contrasting hues for floor vs ceiling so the horizon line is unambiguous.

### FOV, Aspect, and the Implicit Pinhole Camera

- **Behavior**: The renderer is a pure pinhole camera — no lens distortion, no motion blur, no depth of field. The full range of view-angles spans 90° horizontally; the vertical extent of what gets rendered is determined by the viewport's aspect.
- **Rules**:
  - Vertical FOV is implicit: the engine does not parameterize it directly. Instead, a fixed vertical extent equals `viewport_height`, and that maps to a vertical world-space angle whose value depends on aspect.
  - The reference's pixels are non-square (taller than wide at 4:3 output of a 320×200 framebuffer), and the per-row span-step calculation uses `screen_half_width` for both the horizontal projection AND the per-row depth denominator — the asymmetry is therefore baked in. A square-pixel target should scale the per-row depth table by the pixel-aspect ratio to preserve the apparent vertical FOV.
  - View-pitch is not supported in the reference's renderer: the camera always points along the horizon. (Crouching / jumping change the camera's vertical position in the sector, but never the camera's pitch — the horizon is always at row `center_y`.)
- **Constants**:
  - Horizontal FOV: 90° (fixed).
  - Vertical FOV: derived (≈ 60° at 4:3 with non-square pixels; ≈ 75° at 4:3 with square pixels).
- **Feel**: A fixed-pitch camera keeps the projection math simple and makes wall columns truly vertical. Adding view-pitch later requires either Y-shearing the framebuffer (cheap, slightly distorting) or a full perspective-correct vertical projection (expensive, undistorted).

### Max Render Distance / Far Clipping

- **Behavior**: There is no single "max ray distance" parameter in the reference's wall path. The BSP traversal is naturally bounded by the level's geometry, and the colormap table saturates at its last (darkest) entry, so anything beyond a few hundred world units fades to black on its own.
- **Rules**:
  - Wall scale clamp: per-column scale is clamped to `[≈1/256, 64×]` of the unit projection. Below the lower bound the column is too small to draw; above the upper bound the column is treated as "essentially adjacent to the camera." The clamp is the de-facto far clip.
  - Light index clamp: distance band index is clamped to `max - 1`; beyond that distance, additional darkening is invisible (already at the last colormap row).
  - In a grid-DDA implementation a finite max distance is required (otherwise stepping continues forever in an open map), and the natural choices are: (a) clamp by ray length (e.g. 32 tiles), (b) clamp by colormap saturation (whatever distance produces the last band), or (c) both. The reference's BSP needs neither because a finite map terminates the traversal automatically.
- **Constants**:
  - Reasonable far-clip for a 20×15 tile world: 32 tiles is more than the diagonal, so the cap rarely fires; smaller worlds (≤ 16×16) can use 16 tiles safely.
- **Feel**: A correctly-tuned far clip is invisible — the player cannot see "the void" beyond it because the light system has already faded the last visible row to black. A too-aggressive clip produces visible "wall pop-in" at world-edge tiles.

### Wall Traversal Strategy

- **Behavior**: The reference walks wall geometry via a binary space partition tree. The tree is precomputed offline from the level's line-and-sector data; at render time, the camera position determines a front-to-back traversal of the tree. Each visited leaf yields a small set of wall segments to project. Per-column clip arrays (one entry per screen X for "row of lowest wall pixel drawn so far" and one for "row of highest wall pixel drawn so far") track how far down and up walls have already filled each column, so already-occupied pixels are skipped without a depth buffer.
- **Rules**:
  - A node in the tree carries a partition line; "front" and "back" sides relative to the camera are computed by a sign test on the partition equation.
  - Front-to-back order means closer geometry is drawn first; once a column is fully covered, subsequent walls into that column are clipped by the per-column arrays without writing pixels.
  - Sprites (entities) are collected during the BSP traversal and drawn back-to-front in a separate masked pass, so they composite over walls correctly.
- **Alternative — grid-DDA**:
  - For a tile-grid world (the typical simplified prototype), per-column rendering can use a Bresenham-style DDA walk through the tile array: starting from the camera, step the ray one tile boundary at a time (always the *nearer* of the next horizontal vs vertical tile boundary), and stop on the first solid-tile hit. The hit's perpendicular distance comes from the axis the ray last crossed, with no trig.
  - DDA gives painter-style ordering naturally: each column processes its own ray independently, no occlusion array required, no precomputed tree.
  - DDA has no built-in mechanism for sectors with varying floor/ceiling heights (they require either height information per tile or a separate height map), and no support for non-axis-aligned walls.
- **Constants**:
  - DDA cost: one step per tile boundary crossed, so cost per column is O(map diagonal) worst case; a 20×15 map has ≤ 35 steps per column. For 320 columns this is 11 200 steps per frame, trivial.
- **Feel**: Both traversal strategies produce identical projection output up to per-pixel rounding. The choice is dictated by world representation (BSP for arbitrary line+sector data, DDA for tile grids), not by visual outcome.

## Key Insights

- **Column-based first-person view is a projection technique, not a traversal technique.** The per-column scale formula (focal / perpendicular_distance) is the same whether walls come from a BSP tree, a grid DDA walk, a portal renderer, or any other geometry source. A spec for "raycaster renderer" should pin the projection math and leave the traversal as a separate, world-representation-driven choice.
- **Perpendicular distance is the linchpin.** Computed once per wall (BSP) or per ray-axis-crossing (DDA), used unmodified for every column on that wall. Confusing perpendicular distance with ray-distance is the single most common bug in beginner column renderers and produces the classic fisheye bow.
- **Lighting is table lookup, not arithmetic.** A single 2D colormap table, indexed by (sector light, distance band), produces all the per-pixel shading the renderer needs. Floors, ceilings, and walls all share the same table — only the distance-band derivation differs. This is far cheaper than per-pixel multiply-and-add and gives stable banding across the whole frame.
- **NS/EW shading is one integer adjustment per wall.** The "fake directional light" effect costs effectively nothing — a `+1` or `-1` on the light-level index — yet contributes substantially to scene legibility.
- **Floors and ceilings can be drastically simplified without breaking the renderer.** A flat-coloured floor/ceiling pair (one solid colour above the horizon, one below) reuses all wall-column code unchanged; only the per-column "fill the gap" step changes from "for each row, sample the floor texture at world-space offset X" to "for each row, write the floor colour." A consumer that cannot afford textured-flat span filling (cost, memory, asset pipeline) loses no mechanic by going flat-coloured — only perceptual richness.
- **Non-square pixels matter.** The reference's vertical FOV is implicitly slightly squashed by its 4:3-on-non-square-pixels framebuffer; a modern square-pixel target either replicates that squash deliberately (for visual parity) or scales the per-row depth table to make vertical and horizontal angle units match (for "more natural" view).

## Open Questions

- **Sprite/entity rendering** (billboarding, per-column z-test against the wall pass): out of scope for this knowledge entry — covered separately when sprite extraction is requested.
- **First-person weapon overlay (player sprite)**: a separate masked-sprite layer drawn last, composited on top of the world view. Out of scope here; covered in the visual-feedback knowledge.
- **HUD / status bar layout in screen pixels**: covered in the HUD knowledge entry.
- **Crosshair**: the reference has no crosshair (one is implied by autoaim assist). A modern prototype that wants one is making a deliberate addition.
- **View-pitch / vertical look**: not present in the reference's projection; adding it requires Y-shearing or a true perspective Y projection. Belongs in a follow-up knowledge entry if ever specified.
- **Dynamic lights** (point lights, flickering sectors): the reference's "extra light" is a single global integer per frame. Per-light attenuation around individual objects would be a fresh design, not an extraction.

## Deferred

- **Texture mapping for walls**: the reference loads column-major texture patches and steps through them at a per-column inverse-scale rate; a simplified renderer can either solid-fill the column with a per-tile colour, or skip texturing entirely and paint each wall in a constant colour modulated by the distance shade.
- **Texture mapping for floors and ceilings**: see "simplified flat-colour" alternative above; reusing the wall column code with a horizon split is the cheapest possible substitute.
- **Sky as a special floor/ceiling case**: covered briefly above; a flat-colour ceiling can substitute for the sky path.
- **Portal / window walls (two-sided lines)**: the reference's two-sided line case (a wall with a different sector behind it, possibly with a top texture, bottom texture, and middle masked texture) requires per-column floor and ceiling clip arrays and a more careful column loop. A grid-DDA implementation operating on solid/empty tiles needs none of this; doors and windows in such a world are modelled as solid/non-solid tile transitions, not as pixel-clipped walls.
